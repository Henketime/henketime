Detailed Company Profiles

Amazon (AWS)

Data Centers & Expansion

AWS operates 39 global regions and 123 availability zones. Amazon announced a $15 billion investment in Northern Indiana for new AI-optimized data center campuses with multi-gigawatt capacity.

Electricity & Energy

Amazon claims to match 100% of electricity with renewable energy. To meet growing AI demand, Amazon is investing $500 million in X-energy's small modular reactors (SMRs), targeting up to 960 MW of capacity by 2039.

What this means: AWS is committing to firm carbon-free power (nuclear) in addition to variable renewables, which is more credible than renewables-only claims for 24/7 reliability.

Water & Groundwater

AWS states most sites use air cooling with no water much of the year. The company is expanding reclaimed/recycled water cooling to 120+ US data center locations by 2030 and commits to water-positive operations (replenishing more than it consumes).

Objective baseline: Construction sites must comply with EPA NPDES stormwater permits and Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plans (SWPPPs). Facilities storing diesel for backup generators must maintain SPCC secondary containment.

Defense Involvement

AWS is a named awardee in the Department of Defense's JWCC (Joint Warfighting Cloud Capability) contract, which provides cloud services to DoD customers for various applications including warfighting support.

Microsoft (Azure)

Data Centers & Expansion

Microsoft announced a $3.3 billion Wisconsin investment for an AI-focused data center campus plus workforce and community initiatives. The company continues state-level partnerships to expand cloud infrastructure.

Electricity & Energy

Microsoft targets carbon-negative operations by 2030 (removing more carbon than it emits). The strategy combines energy efficiency and large-scale clean electricity procurement as AI demand grows.

Water & Groundwater

Microsoft is deploying direct-to-chip, closed-loop cooling systems designed to eliminate evaporative cooling water use. The company commits to water-positive operations by 2030 in stressed basins.

Key innovation: Closed-loop cooling eliminates the biggest water risk (evaporative cooling), but requires validation against actual operating data and permit compliance records.

Defense Involvement

Microsoft is a JWCC awardee and also holds an active multi-year US Army contract for the IVAS (Integrated Visual Augmentation System) program—a direct military capability development vehicle.

Google (Alphabet / Google Cloud)

Data Centers & Expansion

Google continues global infrastructure scaling to support AI and cloud workloads. The company emphasizes efficiency and clean energy procurement as core design principles.

Electricity & Energy

Google's data center electricity consumption increased 27% year-over-year in 2024 (AI-driven), while reported emissions fell 12%, attributed to new clean energy projects. Google signed the world's first corporate agreement to purchase electricity from multiple SMRs (Kairos Power, up to 500 MW by 2035).

Important distinction: Google differentiates between annual renewable energy matching and 24/7 carbon-free energy (CFE). This is more honest about temporal/geographic mismatches.

Water & Groundwater

Google uses alternative water sources, direct-to-chip cooling, and climate-conscious design. In 2024, water stewardship projects replenished ~64% of the company's freshwater consumption (4.5 billion gallons), spanning 112 projects in 68 watersheds.

Defense Involvement

Google is a JWCC awardee and provides cloud services to DoD customers, which may include warfighting and intelligence support depending on use.

Meta

Data Centers & Expansion

Meta operates a global data center fleet and is building large AI-optimized campuses, including Richland Parish, Louisiana (its largest to date, delivering 2+ GW of compute capacity for open-source LLM training).

Electricity & Energy

Meta claims net zero global operations since 2020 and matches 100% of electricity with renewable energy via a large contracted portfolio. New AI-optimized data centers support liquid-cooled hardware, which sustains upward pressure on electricity demand.

Water & Groundwater

Meta reports water withdrawal, consumption, and efficiency metrics for data centers. The company targets water-positive by 2030 with restoration projects in stressed regions. New designs support liquid cooling, which shifts water risk based on whether cooling is closed-loop.

Defense Involvement

Meta makes its Llama models available to US government agencies and defense contractors via partners including AWS, Microsoft, Oracle, Palantir, and Anduril. This represents an explicit shift toward defense-adjacent deployment pathways.

Why this matters: Llama availability to Anduril (a defense contractor) and other national security partners means Meta's foundation models are being integrated into US military systems.

Electricity & Energy: What the Numbers Mean

The Challenge: AI is power-hungry

AI training and inference consume enormous amounts of electricity. For example, Google reported a 27% year-over-year increase in data center electricity consumption in 2024, driven largely by AI adoption. This creates two ethical questions:

  1. Where does the power come from? (Renewables vs fossil fuels vs nuclear)
  2. Can the grid handle it? (Are these companies helping build new generation, or just buying existing capacity?)

Current Claims

  • Amazon & Meta: Claim 100% renewable energy matching (annual aggregate)
  • Microsoft: Carbon-negative by 2030 (combines efficiency + procurement)
  • Google: Distinguishes between annual matching and 24/7 carbon-free energy (CFE)
  • OpenAI: "Pay its own way" on energy; menu of options including SMRs and gas-with-capture

What's credible?

Red flags to watch for:

  • Claims of 100% renewable energy but no breakdown of capacity, location, or timeline
  • No distinction between annual matching and 24/7 carbon-free supply
  • Renewable energy contracts that are far from the data center (loose geographic nexus)
  • No mention of what happens on low-wind, low-solar days (grid balancing, storage, or firm power?)

What's stronger evidence?

Firm carbon-free power: Amazon's $500M investment in X-energy SMRs and Google's Kairos Power agreement demonstrate willingness to pay for 24/7 carbon-free dispatchable power. This is more credible than renewables-only claims because it addresses the intermittency problem head-on.
Transparency on 24/7 CFE: Google's distinction between "annual matching" and "hourly CFE progress" is more honest than hiding behind aggregate claims.

Water & Groundwater: Local Impact Risk Assessment

Why water matters for data centers

Data centers use water primarily for cooling. Evaporative cooling (cooling towers) can consume significant freshwater, especially in hot climates, and can compete with drinking water supplies or stressed agricultural regions. Contamination risks arise from chemical storage, spills, and wastewater discharge.

Groundwater Contamination: Regulatory Baseline

The strongest "objective" facts about groundwater risk come from regulatory frameworks that apply to all industrial facilities:

NPDES Stormwater Permits: Construction and operation of data centers in areas covered by stormwater discharge regulations require NPDES permit coverage and implementation of Stormwater Pollution Prevention Plans (SWPPPs) with erosion control, sediment filtration, and final stabilization measures.
SPCC (Spill Prevention, Control & Countermeasure): Facilities storing oil above specified thresholds (e.g., diesel for backup generators) must develop SPCC plans, maintain secondary containment, and establish spill response protocols to prevent discharge to navigable waters.
Underground Injection Control (UIC): Some data centers may inject cooling water or other fluids underground; such activities require EPA or state UIC permitting and compliance with drinking water protection standards.

How to verify water practices at a specific site

Due diligence checklist:

  • Water source (potable vs reclaimed) and volume estimates
  • Cooling system type (air, closed-loop, evaporative, hybrid) and capacity
  • Wastewater discharge permit number and effluent limitations
  • Backup generator fuel type, storage volume, secondary containment details
  • NPDES SWPPP implementation and inspection records
  • Spill history and environmental audit findings (if public)
  • Local water stress category (World Resources Institute Water Risk Atlas or USGS data)

How to Verify Claims & Speak with Authority

Primary source hierarchy

To speak credibly, anchor your discussion in this ranking of evidence quality:

  1. Official sustainability reports & audited metrics (e.g., Meta's 2024 Sustainability Report with Ernst & Young assurance; Google's Environmental Report)
  2. Government announcements & contract awards (e.g., DoD JWCC notice; X-energy partnership announcement from Amazon)
  3. Regulatory permits & filings (NPDES stormwater, SPCC plans, utility interconnection agreements)
  4. Company press releases & blog posts (credible but unaudited)
  5. News media reporting (verify against primary sources; watch for editorial spin)

How to build authority in your team

Step 1: Separate marketing from substance

  • Does the company publish absolute electricity use (MWh) or only percentages of renewables?
  • Does it distinguish between annual matching and 24/7 carbon-free energy?
  • Does it disclose water withdrawal AND consumption separately?
  • Does it report environmental incidents (spills, permit violations)?

Step 2: Ask for site-level data before adopting

  • Power Usage Effectiveness (PUE) for the specific region where your data will run
  • Water Usage Effectiveness (WUE) and water source breakdown (potable vs reclaimed)
  • Confirmation of NPDES permit coverage and SWPPP compliance
  • Backup generator capacity and SPCC secondary containment details
  • Written statement on defense-use boundaries for the specific product

Step 3: Document your due diligence

  • Cite the sustainability report (include year and link)
  • Reference government contracts by name and date (e.g., DoD JWCC award, June 2025)
  • Note which metrics are audited vs unaudited (important for credibility)
  • Flag unresolved questions (e.g., "Google reports 12% emission reduction but absolute electricity use rose 27%; reconciliation unclear")

What to do when data isn't available

If a company doesn't publish a specific metric, that's important information. You can say:

  • "Company X does not publicly disclose data center PUE by region, which makes it difficult to assess efficiency for our specific workloads."
  • "Company Y claims 100% renewable energy but does not explain how it balances intermittency on low-wind, low-solar days."
  • "Company Z has a DoD JWCC contract but does not publish written use restrictions or audit trails for military workloads."

Absence of evidence is not evidence of virtue—but it is evidence of opacity, which is itself an ethical concern.
